IDDSI Framework: Quick Reference

The International Dysphagia Diet Standardisation Initiative (IDDSI) provides a globally standardised framework for describing food textures and drink thicknesses. Australian aged care facilities must align to these standards under the Aged Care Quality Standards.

Level Name Type Key Characteristics
0 Thin Drink Water, juice, milk. Flows like water.
1 Slightly Thick Drink Thicker than water, flows through a teat
2 Mildly Thick Drink Pours off a spoon, effort needed to drink from cup
3 Liquidised Drink / Food Smooth, no lumps, can be drunk from cup or syringe
4 Pureed Food Smooth cohesive, no lumps, cannot be piped or molded
5 Minced & Moist Food Small particles (4mm), moist, no separate thin liquid
6 Soft & Bite-Sized Food Tender, can be mashed with tongue, max 15mm pieces
7 Regular / Easy to Chew Food Normal diet with consideration for chewing ability

1. Resident Dietary Profile Documentation

Each resident must have a current, signed dietary profile on file. Review and update at least every 3 months, or when condition changes.

  • Resident IDDSI texture level assigned and documented (Levels 0–7)
  • Texture prescription signed by a Speech-Language Pathologist (SLP) or dietitian
  • All allergens and intolerances documented (including cross-contamination risks)
  • Cultural, religious, and personal food preferences recorded
  • Thickener type and ratio specified for drink modifications
  • Assistance requirements noted (e.g., staff to sit beside, adaptive utensils)
  • Profile reviewed and re-signed after acute illness or hospitalization
  • Next review date scheduled and tracked
  • Resident and/or family informed of and consent to current texture level
  • Profile accessible to kitchen and care staff at point of meal service

2. Meal Ordering & Planning

Meal ordering systems must cross-check resident profiles at point of order to prevent incorrect texture meals reaching residents.

  • Meal ordering process checks resident IDDSI level before confirming order
  • Allergen conflict check performed at order time (not just profile entry)
  • Meals for each texture level clearly differentiated on kitchen production sheets
  • Menu options available at each IDDSI level for all meal periods
  • Fortified or modified meals labeled separately from standard meals
  • Meal counts per texture level communicated to kitchen before each service
  • Process for last-minute dietary changes communicated to kitchen
  • Guest/visitor meals handled without cross-contaminating resident-specific production

3. Kitchen Preparation & Verification

Kitchen staff must be able to verify their output meets IDDSI standards before meals leave the kitchen. Visual spot-checks are insufficient; use standardised testing methods.

  • Fork drip test used for Levels 1–4 drinks and foods
  • Spoon tilt test used for purees and thickened drinks
  • Finger test (pressing food) used for Levels 5–7
  • Particle size checked against IDDSI specifications (e.g. 4mm for Level 5)
  • Thickener preparation follows manufacturer-specified ratios by weight, not by eye
  • Kitchen production records show texture level for each batch prepared
  • Separate equipment/utensils for allergen-modified meals
  • Temperature checks documented before meal delivery (hot >60°C, cold <5°C)
  • Plating standards for each texture level are documented with photos
  • Batch labels on modified meals include texture level and allergen flags

4. Staff Training & Competency

All staff involved in meal ordering, preparation, or service must demonstrate competency in IDDSI. Training records must be current and accessible to auditors.

  • All kitchen and care staff completed IDDSI induction training (document date)
  • Annual IDDSI competency refresher completed for all relevant staff
  • Thickener preparation training completed and logged per staff member
  • Training records include staff name, date, trainer, and competency outcome
  • New staff complete IDDSI training before unsupervised meal service
  • Agency/casual staff briefed on facility-specific IDDSI requirements before shift
  • Escalation pathway known: who to call if unsure about a resident's texture level
  • SLP contact details available to kitchen and care staff

5. Audit & Continuous Improvement

Regular self-auditing against IDDSI standards is evidence of a well-managed quality system and will be reviewed by the Aged Care Quality and Safety Commission.

  • Monthly internal dietary audit conducted and documented
  • Mealtime observation audit: staff observe residents during meals quarterly
  • Incident register captures any texture-related adverse events
  • Resident and family feedback on meals collected and acted on
  • Corrective actions from previous audits are documented and closed out
  • IDDSI policy and procedures reviewed annually
  • Dietitian involvement in menu review at least annually
  • Emergency/evacuation plan accounts for residents requiring modified textures

Regulatory Context

IDDSI compliance in Australian aged care is referenced under the following frameworks:

  • Aged Care Quality Standard 3: Personal care and clinical care (nutrition and hydration)
  • Aged Care Quality Standard 4: Services and supports for daily living (meals and dining)
  • Safe Work Australia: Aspiration and choking risk management
  • IDDSI Framework (2019): iddsi.org, the authoritative reference for all texture level definitions
