Why Dietary Documentation Matters

Under the Aged Care Quality Standards (particularly Standards 1, 3, and 5), providers must demonstrate that residents' nutritional, dietary, and cultural food preferences are identified, recorded, and consistently acted upon. Inspectors look for evidence in both the clinical record and day-to-day operational records.

Poor dietary documentation is one of the most common causes of non-compliance findings. This checklist covers the six core documentation categories that inspectors examine - use it to audit your current practices and identify gaps before an ACQSC assessment.

  Standard 1 - Consumer Dignity and Choice

Residents' food preferences, cultural and religious requirements, and dislikes must be documented and reflected in menu planning and meal delivery. Evidence of choice and self-determination is assessed.

  Standard 3 - Personal Care and Clinical Care

Nutritional needs must be assessed, risks (including malnutrition) identified, and outcomes monitored. Includes MUST screening, weight records, dietitian referrals, IDDSI compliance, and allergen management.

  Standard 5 - Organisation's Service Environment

The environment in which food is prepared and delivered must be safe. This includes documented allergy management processes, kitchen IDDSI preparation protocols, and staff training records.

1. Resident Dietary Profile

Every resident must have a current dietary profile on admission and updated whenever their needs change. This is the foundation document from which all meal planning, ordering, and allergen management flows.

  • Solid food texture requirement (IDDSI level)
    Must use the correct IDDSI terminology (e.g., Level 5 Minced & Moist, Level 6 Soft & Bite-Sized) - not informal descriptors like "soft" or "chopped"
  • Fluid texture requirement (IDDSI level)
    e.g., Level 2 Mildly Thick, Level 3 Moderately Thick - must be assessed by a Speech Pathologist for levels 1–4
  • Documented allergens with clinical source
    Distinguish medically confirmed allergies from intolerances and preferences; source noted (self-reported, medical record, GP letter)
  • Dietary restrictions (medical/clinical)
    e.g., diabetic diet, renal diet, low-FODMAP - must reference the clinical basis (GP or dietitian recommendation)
  • Food preferences, cultural and religious requirements
    e.g., Halal, Kosher, vegan, country of origin preferences - documented as resident-reported
  • Food dislikes
    Often overlooked - should be documented to prevent repeated meal rejections that can affect intake
  • Oral health and dentition notes relevant to texture
    e.g., full dentures, missing posterior teeth, recent extraction - informs texture level choice
  • Profile review date and responsible staff member
    Must be reviewed at minimum quarterly, or after any clinical event affecting eating/swallowing

2. Allergen Management Documentation

Allergen errors in aged care can be fatal. Documentation must be comprehensive, accessible at point of care, and built into the ordering and meal delivery workflow.

  • Allergen register - all residents with confirmed allergies listed
    Should be accessible by kitchen staff at all times, not just in clinical notes
  • Allergen information recorded against each menu item
    Kitchen must know which dishes contain which allergens before service, not after
  • Conflict checks performed before orders are placed
    Process for identifying when a menu item conflicts with a resident's allergen profile - ideally at ordering stage
  • Tray labels or delivery cards indicating allergen requirements
    Visible allergen alerts at point of delivery to reduce risk of wrong meal being served
  • Staff training records for allergen awareness
    Both kitchen and care staff must be trained; records must show completion dates and staff names
  • Incident report for any allergen exposure event
    Even near-misses must be documented, investigated, and actioned

3. Meal Ordering & Delivery Records

Facilities must be able to demonstrate that meals ordered and delivered match the resident's care plan requirements. This is frequently checked against intake records during audits.

  • Daily meal orders recorded per resident per meal period
    Records must show what was ordered - not just that a meal was served - to allow retrospective dietary review
  • Delivery confirmation recorded (confirmed/delivered status)
    Separate from ordering - confirms the resident received the meal, not just that it was prepared
  • Food intake percentage recorded at each meal
    Required for MUST medium/high risk residents; best practice for all; supports early identification of declining intake
  • Meal refusals documented with reason
    Repeated refusals must be escalated to clinical lead and noted in care plan - not just left as a blank record
  • Waste notes for low-intake meals
    Descriptive note of what was left and any resident comments - valuable clinical context beyond a percentage figure
  • Supplementary nutrition intake recorded (oral supplements, fortified foods)
    If supplements are prescribed, their intake must also be recorded - often an audit gap
  • Between-meal snacks and fluid intake records where clinically indicated
    Relevant for dehydration risk, fluid-thickening compliance, and MUST medium/high residents

4. Care Plan - Nutrition & Dietary Section

The care plan nutrition section must translate the resident's dietary profile into specific, measurable goals and actions. Inspectors look for evidence that the care plan is current, individualised, and reflected in practice.

  • Documented nutritional goals (calorie and protein targets)
    Minimum 30 kcal/kg/day and 1.0–1.35 g protein/kg/day (ESPEN 2022); goals must be individualised, not generic
  • IDDSI texture level specified and rationale documented
    Care plan must state the level (not just "soft diet") and reference the assessment that determined it (e.g., SLP assessment dated DD/MM/YYYY)
  • Current MUST score and risk category recorded
    Must be updated after each screening - not just at admission
  • Dietitian involvement documented where applicable
    Date of referral, date seen, and any dietitian recommendations must appear in both the dietitian note and the care plan
  • Actions for poor intake or weight loss specified
    e.g., "If intake drops below 50% at two consecutive meals, notify care coordinator" - must be a specific escalation pathway
  • Care plan review date and responsible clinician
    Minimum quarterly review; after hospitalisation; after any significant change in condition
  • Resident (or representative) signature confirming goals discussed
    Demonstrates informed consent and person-centred care - required under Standard 1

5. Weight & Clinical Monitoring Records

Weight is a primary indicator of nutritional status in aged care. All weight records must be attributable, dated, and linked to a clinical response when thresholds are crossed.

  • Monthly weight recorded on calibrated scales
    Paper-based records must note scale ID and calibration date; digital systems should record timestamp and staff name automatically
  • Weight trend tracked over time (minimum 3 months visible)
    A single weight reading is insufficient - inspectors want to see trend data to assess trajectory
  • Unplanned weight loss of ≥ 3 kg or ≥ 5% flagged and actioned
    Documentation must show the flagging, who was notified, what was done, and the timeline - a "clinical concern" note alone is insufficient
  • 3-day food and fluid diary completed for medium/high MUST risk
    Must cover three consecutive days including a weekend day; completed by care staff at point of care
  • Incident reports for clinical nutrition events (choking, aspiration, severe refusal)
    Every incident must be reported, investigated, and linked back to care plan update
  • MUST screening repeated after hospitalisation or acute illness
    Residents returning from hospital commonly have changed nutritional status - a new MUST score must be completed within 48 hours of return

6. Kitchen & Menu Documentation

Kitchen documentation ensures that meal planning is nutritionally adequate, IDDSI-compliant, and consistent with residents' dietary profiles. This is often the weakest documentation area in many facilities.

  • Weekly or fortnightly menu in advance and accessible to residents
    Residents must be able to make informed choices - menus should be available at least one day before the meal period
  • Menu reviewed by dietitian (minimum annually, or when significantly changed)
    Dietitian sign-off date must be documented on the menu or in the governance record
  • Allergen content of each menu item documented in kitchen records
    Kitchen must have ingredient-level allergen information - labelling on tins/packets is not sufficient for multi-component dishes
  • IDDSI texture preparation instructions for each menu item at each texture level
    Must describe the preparation method, not just the target level - e.g., "blend X with Y until smooth, pass through sieve" for Level 4 Pureed
  • Portion sizes and caloric estimates documented
    Supports nutritional compliance reporting and dietitian review; essential for ESPEN 2022 calorie target verification
  • Substitute options documented for each meal period
    What alternative is offered when a resident refuses the menu item or it conflicts with their dietary profile?
  • Kitchen prep checklist completed and signed daily
    Confirms IDDSI textures were prepared correctly before service; must be retained for audit

Most Common Audit Gaps

These are the dietary documentation failures most frequently cited in ACQSC non-compliance findings.

  • Dietary profiles not updated after hospitalisation or clinical change - admission profile treated as permanent
  • IDDSI levels stated in informal terms ("soft", "minced") rather than the standardised IDDSI framework level names
  • Allergen records in clinical notes only - kitchen staff cannot access them at point of food preparation
  • Meal delivery records showing orders placed but no intake or delivery confirmation
  • Care plan nutrition goals stated generically ("maintain weight") rather than with specific, measurable targets
  • Weight loss flagged in a progress note but no care plan update or dietitian referral documented as a result
  • Menu reviewed by dietitian but no sign-off date or dietitian name recorded on the menu document
  • Fluid-thickening orders not matched against kitchen prep records - thickened fluids prepared inconsistently
  • No escalation pathway documented - staff know to flag poor intake but there is no written procedure specifying who, when, and how
  • Cultural or religious dietary requirements noted at admission but not reflected in the weekly menu or meal order system
