What is Food Cost Percentage?

Food cost percentage tells you what proportion of your revenue goes toward the ingredients used to make a dish. It is one of the most important metrics for any restaurant owner to track.

Food Cost % = (Cost of Ingredients / Selling Price) × 100

Applied to each dish, or to the whole business using COGS / Total Revenue

For example: if a burger costs $4.50 in ingredients and sells for $18, the food cost is 25%. That means 75 cents of every dollar goes to labour, rent, power, and profit.

Most restaurateurs track this at two levels: per dish (to price the menu correctly) and across the whole business (to spot category problems and supplier price creep).

Australian Restaurant Benchmarks

Food cost targets vary by segment. Use these as a starting point for your own business.

Restaurant type Target food cost % Status
Fine dining 25–30% Healthy
Casual dining / bistro 28–32% Healthy
Cafe / brunch 25–30% Healthy
Quick service / takeaway 28–35% Watch
Food trucks / pop-ups 30–38% Watch
Anything above 40% Check your pricing and suppliers Action needed
Keep in mind: food cost % alone does not tell the full story. A high-volume dish at 35% can be more profitable than a low-volume dish at 22%. Track both margin and sales volume together.

How to Calculate the Cost of a Dish

Costing a recipe takes five steps. Walk through it once for each dish on your menu, then update it whenever ingredient prices change.

  1. 1
    List every ingredient with exact quantities

    Include everything: proteins, produce, sauces, garnishes, cooking oil. Use the actual portion size served, not the pack size you buy.

  2. 2
    Calculate the cost per unit for each ingredient

    Divide the purchase price by the quantity in the pack. A 5 kg bag of flour at $8.50 = $1.70 per kg. A 6 L tin of tomatoes at $12 = $2.00 per litre.

  3. 3
    Multiply quantity by unit cost for each ingredient

    If a dish uses 200 g of chicken at $14/kg, that ingredient costs $2.80. Repeat for every item on the list.

  4. 4
    Add a waste factor (typically 5–15%)

    Raw chicken loses around 20% in cooking. Whole fish loses 50%+ in filleting. Apply a trim/shrink factor to your raw costs so you are costing what actually lands on the plate.

  5. 5
    Sum the ingredient costs to get cost per portion

    Divide by your yield if you batch-cook (e.g. a 10-portion bolognese costs $38 to make = $3.80 per serve).

Example: Grilled Chicken Burger

Chicken breast (180g, after shrink) $2.88
Brioche bun $0.75
Lettuce, tomato, pickle $0.55
Sauce (aioli + mustard) $0.22
Chips (150g frozen) $0.90
Oil, seasoning, garnish $0.18
Total cost per serve $4.48

Selling price: $22.00  |  Food cost: 20.4%  |  Rating: Excellent

Menu Engineering: Rank Your Dishes

Menu engineering is the practice of ranking every dish by two dimensions: how profitable it is, and how often it sells. The result tells you exactly what to promote, reprice, or drop.

Stars

High margin, high popularity. Your best dishes. Feature these prominently on the menu and in marketing. Never discount them.

Plowhorses

Low margin, high popularity. Customers love them but they are not making you much money. Try raising the price by $1–2 or reducing portion cost slightly.

Puzzles

High margin, low popularity. Good margins but not ordered often. Reposition on the menu, retrain staff to recommend them, or rewrite the description.

Dogs

Low margin, low popularity. Usually candidates for removal. Keep only if they serve a strategic purpose (e.g. dietary requirements, table anchor item).

To classify your dishes: calculate gross profit per dish (not food cost %), rank by sales volume over 4–8 weeks, then plot each item. Anything above the average gross profit is "high margin"; anything above average sales volume is "high popularity".

Common Mistakes That Push Food Cost Up

Most food cost problems come from one of five causes. Each has a practical fix.

1. Portion drift. Staff eyeball portions instead of weighing them. A 20 g difference on every chicken dish adds up fast across a service. Fix: use a scale for proteins, a ladle guide for sauces, and run spot checks weekly.

2. Pricing off memory, not calculation. Menus are priced when you open and never revisited. If chicken, eggs, or avocado have gone up 30%, your food cost % has risen even if your prices have not. Fix: re-cost the menu every quarter.

3. Supplier price creep. Invoices drift slightly higher each delivery without anyone noticing. Fix: compare invoice prices against your purchase orders every week. A 5% variance threshold is a reasonable trigger for review.

4. Over-ordering and spoilage. Ordering too much leads to waste that never makes it to the plate. Fix: track par levels for your top-20 ingredients and order to restock to par, not to fill the cool room.

5. No recipe cards. When dishes are made from memory, consistency and costing both suffer. Fix: write a recipe card for every dish with gram weights, method, and plating notes. This also speeds up training.

Frequently Asked Questions

What food cost % should I target?

For most Australian casual dining restaurants, 28–32% is the target. Cafes and fine dining typically aim for 25–30%. Food trucks often run higher at 30–38% due to lower labour costs offsetting the margin. What matters most is that your blended food cost % leaves enough room after labour, rent, and overheads for profit.

How often should I re-cost my menu?

At minimum, quarterly. In practice, whenever a key ingredient (protein, dairy, seasonal produce) has a significant price change. If you are buying from two or more suppliers, check for price drift monthly.

Do I need to cost every single menu item?

Yes, but start with your top 10 by sales volume since those have the biggest impact on your overall food cost. Once those are accurate, work through the rest of the menu.

Should I include packaging in my food cost?

For dine-in: no, packaging is usually tracked as a separate supply cost. For takeaway and delivery: yes, include packaging in the recipe cost since it is a direct cost of producing that item for sale.

What is the difference between food cost % and gross profit margin?

Food cost % tells you what percentage of the selling price goes to ingredients. Gross profit margin is 100% minus food cost % (plus other direct costs). Both are useful. Food cost % is more practical for day-to-day kitchen management; gross profit margin is more useful for financial reporting.
