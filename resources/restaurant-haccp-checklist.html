What is HACCP?

HACCP stands for Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points. It is a systematic, preventive approach to food safety that identifies where hazards can occur during food preparation and puts controls in place to prevent them.

In Australia, HACCP principles underpin Standard 3.2.1 of the Food Standards Code (Food Safety Programs), which is mandatory for some food businesses and strongly recommended for all others. Businesses that supply food to hospitals, aged care facilities, childcare centres, or certain retail chains are typically required to have a documented food safety program based on HACCP principles.

Even if you are not legally required to have a formal food safety program, following HACCP principles reduces the risk of a food safety incident, protects your customers, and provides documented evidence that you are managing food safety properly if an incident does occur.

Key legislation: Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ) Food Standards Code, Standard 3.2.1 (Food Safety Programs), Standard 3.2.2 (Food Safety Practices and General Requirements), Standard 3.2.3 (Food Premises and Equipment). Your state or territory may have additional requirements.

Temperature Reference Guide

Temperature control is the most critical HACCP tool for most restaurant kitchens. Food must be kept out of the danger zone (5°C to 60°C) where bacteria multiply rapidly.

Zone Temperature Requirement
Cold storage (fridges) 0°C to 5°C Check twice daily. Log deviations immediately.
Freezer storage -18°C or below Check daily. Any product above -15°C needs assessment.
Hot holding 60°C or above Check every 2 hours during service. Discard after 4 hours.
Cooking (poultry, mince, sausages) 75°C core (minimum) Probe the thickest part. Hold for at least 15 seconds.
Cooking (whole muscle beef, lamb) 63°C core Rest for 3 minutes minimum after reaching target.
Cooling (cooked food) 60°C to 21°C in 2 hours Then from 21°C to 5°C within a further 4 hours.
Danger zone 5°C to 60°C Bacteria double every 20 minutes. Maximum 4 hours total exposure for high-risk foods.

Critical Control Points for Restaurants

A Critical Control Point (CCP) is a step in your process where a control measure is essential to prevent or eliminate a food safety hazard. Most restaurant kitchens have six key CCPs.

1. Receiving deliveries

Check temperatures of refrigerated goods on arrival. Reject anything above 5°C (or above 0°C for seafood). Check use-by dates and packaging integrity.

2. Cold storage

Maintain 0°C to 5°C. Raw proteins on the bottom shelf, ready-to-eat foods on top. Log fridge temperatures twice daily. Rotate stock (FIFO).

3. Cooking

Reach minimum safe internal temperatures for all proteins. Use a calibrated probe thermometer. Record the temperature and time for high-risk items.

4. Hot holding

Keep hot food at 60°C or above throughout service. Check every 2 hours. Do not mix fresh and held batches. Discard after 4 hours of total holding time.

5. Cooling

Cool cooked food rapidly. Use shallow trays, blast chillers, or ice baths. 60°C to 21°C within 2 hours. 21°C to 5°C within a further 4 hours. Label with date/time.

6. Service and display

Separate raw and ready-to-eat foods. Use separate tongs and utensils. Monitor buffet and display temperatures. Remove items that have been out for more than 4 hours.

Daily HACCP Checklist

Print and complete this checklist at the start and end of each service. Keep completed records for a minimum of 12 months.

Opening checks

Check and record temperature of all fridges and cool rooms Target: 0°C to 5°C. Log the actual reading and initials.
Check and record freezer temperature Target: -18°C or below.
Check use-by and best-before dates on all high-risk items in storage Remove and discard any expired stock.
Verify stock is stored correctly (raw proteins below ready-to-eat foods, all covered and labelled)
Check that probe thermometers are calibrated and in ice water reading 0°C Calibrate with boiling water (100°C) or ice water (0°C) at least weekly.
Confirm handwashing stations are stocked (soap, paper towel, warm running water)
Confirm sanitiser spray bottles are labelled, in date, and at correct concentration

During service

Check and log temperature of hot-held items every 2 hours Target: 60°C or above. Discard if below for more than 30 minutes.
Record internal temperature of all high-risk cooked proteins (poultry, mince, pork, sausages)
Ensure cross-contamination controls are in place (separate boards, utensils, and surfaces for raw and ready-to-eat)
Confirm staff are washing hands after handling raw protein, touching their face, or returning from breaks

Closing checks

Check and record end-of-service fridge and cool room temperatures
Label all prepared items with date of preparation and use-by date (maximum 3 days for most high-risk foods)
Dispose of any food that has exceeded its time-temperature limits
Sanitise all food contact surfaces, including benches, slicers, and chopping boards
Store cleaning cloths in sanitising solution or replace with fresh ones
Ensure all bins are emptied and bin areas are clean
Sign and file completed daily log

Weekly Cleaning and Sanitation Schedule

Deep cleaning tasks that must be completed and documented each week. Assign a responsible staff member and record the completion date and initials.

Weekly tasks

Clean and sanitise fridge and cool room interiors (shelves, door seals, handles)
Degrease and clean oven, grill, and fryer interiors
Clean and sanitise dishwasher interior, filters, and spray arms
Descale coffee machine (if applicable) and clean milk frother lines
Clean rangehood filters (remove and degrease with hot water)
Sanitise all food prep areas including underneath and behind equipment
Check pest control stations and log observations
Inspect dry storage areas for signs of moisture, pests, or damaged packaging

Monthly tasks

Professional rangehood and duct cleaning (or every 3 months for high-volume kitchens)
Calibrate and test all probe thermometers against a reference standard
Review and update allergen menu documentation
Conduct food safety training refresher with all kitchen staff
Review supplier food safety certificates (letters of guarantee)

Records You Must Keep

If you have a documented food safety program, your records are what prove it is working. Local councils and state agencies can ask to see these records during an inspection.

Temperature logs. Date, time, equipment name, recorded temperature, and staff initials. Keep for at least 12 months.

Cooking and cooling records. For high-risk items: dish name, batch size, start temperature, end temperature, and time achieved. Particularly important for catering and high-volume production.

Cleaning completion records. Daily, weekly, and monthly cleaning tasks with date, staff member, and sign-off. A simple initials sheet works fine.

Delivery acceptance records. Date, supplier, goods received, temperature on arrival, and acceptance or rejection decision.

Staff food safety training records. Names, dates, and certificates for Food Handler training. All food handlers in Australia must have completed food safety training appropriate to their role.

Corrective action records. Any time you identify a problem (out-of-range temperature, pest sighting, contamination event), document what happened and what you did about it.

Digital vs paper: both are acceptable. Digital logs with timestamps (such as those in RestroAI) are easier to retrieve during an inspection and harder to lose. Whatever format you use, the records must be accurate, legible, and accessible.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do all Australian restaurants need a HACCP plan?

Not all businesses are legally required to have a formal HACCP plan under Standard 3.2.1. However, all food businesses must comply with Standard 3.2.2 (Food Safety Practices), which covers most of the practical requirements described in this checklist. Check with your local council environmental health officer to confirm what applies to your premises and business type.

What food handler training is required in Australia?

Standard 3.2.2A (which commenced in December 2023) requires at least one person in a food business to be a trained Food Safety Supervisor (FSS) holding a Nationally Recognised Qualification. All other food handlers must also have food safety skills and knowledge appropriate to their role. Training requirements vary slightly by state and territory.

How long do I need to keep food safety records?

Standard 3.2.1 requires records to be kept for a minimum of 12 months. Some businesses keep them for 24 months as a precaution, particularly if they supply other businesses (catering, meal kits, aged care).

What happens if my fridge temperature goes out of range overnight?

First, check whether the deviation was brief or extended. If a fridge was found at 8°C in the morning, assess how long it was above 5°C. Foods that have spent a cumulative 4 hours or more in the danger zone (5°C to 60°C) should generally be discarded. Document the incident, what you discarded, and what corrective action you took (repair, restock, temporary use of another fridge).

Do I need a separate cutting board for raw meat and vegetables?

Yes. Cross-contamination from raw proteins (particularly poultry) to ready-to-eat foods is one of the most common causes of food poisoning outbreaks. Use colour-coded boards (red for raw meat, green for vegetables, yellow for poultry, blue for raw seafood) and sanitise between use. Replace boards that are heavily scored since deep grooves cannot be adequately sanitised.
