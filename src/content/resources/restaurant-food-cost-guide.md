<h2><i class="fas fa-percent"></i> What is Food Cost Percentage?</h2>
<p class="section-intro">Food cost percentage tells you what proportion of your revenue goes toward the ingredients used to make a dish. It is one of the most important metrics for any restaurant owner to track.</p>
<div class="formula-block">
<div class="formula">Food Cost % = (Cost of Ingredients / Selling Price) &times; 100</div>
<p>Applied to each dish, or to the whole business using COGS / Total Revenue</p>
</div>
<p style="font-size:0.92rem;color:var(--dark);line-height:1.7">For example: if a burger costs $4.50 in ingredients and sells for $18, the food cost is 25%. That means 75 cents of every dollar goes to labour, rent, power, and profit.</p>
<p style="font-size:0.92rem;color:var(--dark);line-height:1.7;margin-top:1rem">Most restaurateurs track this at two levels: per dish (to price the menu correctly) and across the whole business (to spot category problems and supplier price creep).</p>
</div>

<h2><i class="fas fa-list-ol"></i> How to Calculate the Cost of a Dish</h2>
<p class="section-intro">Costing a recipe takes five steps. Walk through it once for each dish on your menu, then update it whenever ingredient prices change.</p>
<ol class="step-list">
<li><div class="step-number">1</div><div class="step-content"><strong>List every ingredient with exact quantities</strong><p>Include everything: proteins, produce, sauces, garnishes, cooking oil. Use the actual portion size served, not the pack size you buy.</p></div></li>
<li><div class="step-number">2</div><div class="step-content"><strong>Calculate the cost per unit for each ingredient</strong><p>Divide the purchase price by the quantity in the pack. A 5 kg bag of flour at $8.50 = $1.70 per kg. A 6 L tin of tomatoes at $12 = $2.00 per litre.</p></div></li>
<li><div class="step-number">3</div><div class="step-content"><strong>Multiply quantity by unit cost for each ingredient</strong><p>If a dish uses 200 g of chicken at $14/kg, that ingredient costs $2.80. Repeat for every item on the list.</p></div></li>
<li><div class="step-number">4</div><div class="step-content"><strong>Add a waste factor (typically 5&ndash;15%)</strong><p>Raw chicken loses around 20% in cooking. Whole fish loses 50%+ in filleting. Apply a trim/shrink factor to your raw costs so you are costing what actually lands on the plate.</p></div></li>
<li><div class="step-number">5</div><div class="step-content"><strong>Sum the ingredient costs to get cost per portion</strong><p>Divide by your yield if you batch-cook (e.g. a 10-portion bolognese costs $38 to make = $3.80 per serve).</p></div></li>
</ol>

<h2><i class="fas fa-grid-2"></i> Menu Engineering: Rank Your Dishes</h2>
<p class="section-intro">Menu engineering is the practice of ranking every dish by two dimensions: how profitable it is, and how often it sells. The result tells you exactly what to promote, reprice, or drop.</p>
<div class="matrix-grid">
<div class="matrix-card stars"><h4><i class="fas fa-star"></i> Stars</h4><p>High margin, high popularity. Your best dishes. Feature these prominently on the menu and in marketing. Never discount them.</p></div>
<div class="matrix-card plowhorses"><h4><i class="fas fa-horse"></i> Plowhorses</h4><p>Low margin, high popularity. Customers love them but they are not making you much money. Try raising the price by $1&ndash;2 or reducing portion cost slightly.</p></div>
<div class="matrix-card puzzles"><h4><i class="fas fa-puzzle-piece"></i> Puzzles</h4><p>High margin, low popularity. Good margins but not ordered often. Reposition on the menu, retrain staff to recommend them, or rewrite the description.</p></div>
<div class="matrix-card dogs"><h4><i class="fas fa-dog"></i> Dogs</h4><p>Low margin, low popularity. Usually candidates for removal. Keep only if they serve a strategic purpose (e.g. dietary requirements, table anchor item).</p></div>
</div>
<div class="tip-box tip-box-mint" style="margin-top:1.5rem"><strong>To classify your dishes:</strong> calculate gross profit per dish (not food cost %), rank by sales volume over 4&ndash;8 weeks, then plot each item. Anything above the average gross profit is "high margin"; anything above average sales volume is "high popularity".</div>
</div>

<div class="content-section">
<h2><i class="fas fa-triangle-exclamation"></i> Common Mistakes That Push Food Cost Up</h2>
<p class="section-intro">Most food cost problems come from one of five causes. Each has a practical fix.</p>
<div style="font-size:0.9rem;color:var(--dark);line-height:1.8">
<p><strong>1. Portion drift.</strong> Staff eyeball portions instead of weighing them. A 20 g difference on every chicken dish adds up fast across a service. Fix: use a scale for proteins, a ladle guide for sauces, and run spot checks weekly.</p>
<p style="margin-top:1rem"><strong>2. Pricing off memory, not calculation.</strong> Menus are priced when you open and never revisited. If chicken, eggs, or avocado have gone up 30%, your food cost % has risen even if your prices have not. Fix: re-cost the menu every quarter.</p>
<p style="margin-top:1rem"><strong>3. Supplier price creep.</strong> Invoices drift slightly higher each delivery without anyone noticing. Fix: compare invoice prices against your purchase orders every week. A 5% variance threshold is a reasonable trigger for review.</p>
<p style="margin-top:1rem"><strong>4. Over-ordering and spoilage.</strong> Ordering too much leads to waste that never makes it to the plate. Fix: track par levels for your top-20 ingredients and order to restock to par, not to fill the cool room.</p>
<p style="margin-top:1rem"><strong>5. No recipe cards.</strong> When dishes are made from memory, consistency and costing both suffer. Fix: write a recipe card for every dish with gram weights, method, and plating notes. This also speeds up training.</p>
</div>

<h2><i class="fas fa-circle-question"></i> Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<div class="faq-item"><strong>What food cost % should I target?</strong><p>For most Australian casual dining restaurants, 28&ndash;32% is the target. Cafes and fine dining typically aim for 25&ndash;30%. Food trucks often run higher at 30&ndash;38% due to lower labour costs offsetting the margin. What matters most is that your blended food cost % leaves enough room after labour, rent, and overheads for profit.</p></div>
<div class="faq-item"><strong>How often should I re-cost my menu?</strong><p>At minimum, quarterly. In practice, whenever a key ingredient (protein, dairy, seasonal produce) has a significant price change. If you are buying from two or more suppliers, check for price drift monthly.</p></div>
<div class="faq-item"><strong>Do I need to cost every single menu item?</strong><p>Yes, but start with your top 10 by sales volume since those have the biggest impact on your overall food cost. Once those are accurate, work through the rest of the menu.</p></div>
<div class="faq-item"><strong>Should I include packaging in my food cost?</strong><p>For dine-in: no, packaging is usually tracked as a separate supply cost. For takeaway and delivery: yes, include packaging in the recipe cost since it is a direct cost of producing that item for sale.</p></div>
<div class="faq-item"><strong>What is the difference between food cost % and gross profit margin?</strong><p>Food cost % tells you what percentage of the selling price goes to ingredients. Gross profit margin is 100% minus food cost % (plus other direct costs). Both are useful. Food cost % is more practical for day-to-day kitchen management; gross profit margin is more useful for financial reporting.</p></div>
</div>
